Constitution of the Principality of Rupertland

The Constitution of the Principality of Rupertland is the fundamental law establishing the governmental structure, citizens' rights, and basic principles of our nation.

Constitutional Facts

  • Adopted: March 15, 2019
  • Last amended: January 12, 2023 (Environmental Protection Enhancement)
  • Articles: 85 total
  • Languages: English and French (official versions)
  • Status: Supreme law of Rupertland

Download complete Constitution (PDF, 1.2MB)

Constitutional Principle: "The Principality of Rupertland shall be a constitutional monarchy committed to democratic governance, environmental sustainability, digital innovation, and the welfare of all citizens." - Article 1

Structure of the Constitution

Part I: Fundamental Rights and Freedoms (Articles 1-20)

Establishes the fundamental rights and freedoms of all persons within Rupertland, including both citizens and visitors.

Key Rights Protected:

  • Article 2: Right to life, liberty, and security of person
  • Article 3: Freedom of expression, assembly, and religion
  • Article 4: Right to privacy and data protection
  • Article 5: Right to a clean and healthy environment
  • Article 6: Equality before the law and freedom from discrimination
  • Article 7: Right to education and healthcare
  • Article 8: Right to participate in government and democratic processes

Part II: Structure of Government (Articles 21-40)

Defines the basic structure of government including the separation of powers and the relationship between different branches of government.

Government Structure:

  • Article 21: Constitutional monarchy with democratic parliamentary system
  • Article 22: Separation of executive, legislative, and judicial powers
  • Article 23: Role of the Crown as Head of State
  • Article 24: Prime Minister as Head of Government
  • Article 25: Cabinet collective responsibility

Part III: Parliamentary Democracy (Articles 41-55)

Establishes the Parliament of Rupertland, electoral system, and legislative processes.

Parliamentary Provisions:

  • Article 41: Parliament composed of 12 elected representatives
  • Article 42: Universal suffrage for citizens aged 16 and over
  • Article 43: Four-year parliamentary terms
  • Article 44: Legislative process and bill passage procedures
  • Article 45: Parliamentary immunity and privileges

Part IV: The Crown and Executive (Articles 56-65)

Defines the role of the Crown, executive powers, and the relationship between the monarch and elected government.

Executive Authority:

  • Article 56: Crown as constitutional monarch and Head of State
  • Article 57: Executive authority exercised by Prime Minister and Cabinet
  • Article 58: Royal assent required for all legislation
  • Article 59: Appointment of Prime Minister by the Crown
  • Article 60: Ceremonial and representational duties of the Crown

Part V: Judicial System (Articles 66-75)

Establishes an independent judicial system and defines the structure and powers of courts.

Judicial Independence:

  • Article 66: Independent judiciary free from political interference
  • Article 67: Supreme Court as highest court of appeal
  • Article 68: Appointment of judges by the Crown on advice of Parliament
  • Article 69: Security of tenure for judges
  • Article 70: Constitutional review and judicial interpretation

Part VI: Environmental Protection (Articles 76-80)

Unique constitutional provisions establishing environmental protection as a fundamental principle of government (enhanced in 2023 amendment).

Environmental Commitments:

  • Article 76: Constitutional right to a clean and healthy environment
  • Article 77: Government duty to protect natural resources
  • Article 78: Commitment to carbon neutrality and renewable energy
  • Article 79: Environmental impact assessment for all major projects
  • Article 80: Future generations' environmental rights

Part VII: Amendment Procedures (Articles 81-85)

Sets out procedures for constitutional amendment to ensure democratic participation while maintaining constitutional stability.

Amendment Process:

  • Article 81: Two-thirds parliamentary majority required for amendments
  • Article 82: Public consultation required for major amendments
  • Article 83: Royal assent required for constitutional changes
  • Article 84: Protection of fundamental rights from amendment
  • Article 85: Constitutional review every 10 years

Constitutional Principles

Core Values

  • Democratic Governance: Government by consent of the people through elected representatives
  • Constitutional Monarchy: Limited monarchy with democratic accountability
  • Rule of Law: All persons and institutions subject to law
  • Environmental Stewardship: Protection of natural resources for future generations
  • Digital Innovation: Embracing technology for better governance
  • International Cooperation: Peaceful relations and global citizenship
  • Social Equality: Equal rights and opportunities for all

Constitutional History

Development Timeline

  • 2018: Constitutional Assembly convened following the foundation of Rupertland
  • 2019, March 15: Constitution adopted by Constitutional Assembly and ratified by Prince Rupert
  • 2021: First constitutional review completed with minor technical amendments
  • 2023, January 12: Environmental Protection Enhancement amendment passed
  • 2025: Scheduled constitutional review in progress

Constitutional Assembly

The original Constitutional Assembly included 15 founding members representing various professional, academic, and community backgrounds. The Assembly worked for 8 months to draft the Constitution, incorporating extensive public consultation and international best practices.

How the Constitution Works

Supremacy of the Constitution

The Constitution is the supreme law of Rupertland. All other laws, government actions, and decisions must comply with constitutional provisions. The Supreme Court has the authority to determine whether laws or actions are constitutional.

Checks and Balances

  • Parliament makes laws but must respect constitutional limits
  • Government implements laws but is accountable to Parliament
  • Courts interpret laws and ensure constitutional compliance
  • Crown provides stability and continuity while remaining politically neutral

Constitutional Rights in Practice

Citizens can enforce their constitutional rights through the courts. If you believe your constitutional rights have been violated:

  1. Contact a qualified lawyer for legal advice
  2. Consider filing a complaint with relevant government departments
  3. Apply to the courts for constitutional review if necessary
  4. Contact the Ombudsman for administrative complaints

Free legal aid is available for constitutional cases.
